# 1. Why Music Recommendation Needs a New Model

We are now entering an era in which AI has become part of everyday infrastructure.  
Across the technology industry, companies are rapidly integrating conversational AI into their products.

For example:

- **Google** has begun integrating conversational AI into its search engine.
- **Microsoft** has integrated AI assistants into development tools such as **Visual Studio Code**.

This trend represents a fundamental shift in how users interact with software systems.

The same transformation will inevitably occur in **music playback and recommendation software**.  
A music player that incorporates conversational AI is fundamentally different from one that does not. The difference is not incremental but structural.

Therefore, music streaming and playback platforms will also enter the era of **conversational AI-driven recommendation systems**. This transition is unavoidable.

---

## The Fundamental Change Introduced by Conversational AI

Once conversational AI is introduced, the nature of recommendation systems changes dramatically.

The key reason is that a new concept appears:

**the ability to communicate with the recommendation model itself.**

From the user's perspective, this creates a fundamental shift.

In the past:

- Music recommendations were generated by a **background algorithm**.
- The user had **no ability to interact with or negotiate with the model**.
- The system simply presented recommendations without explanation.

In the new paradigm:

- Recommendations are delivered by a **conversational agent that behaves like an entity**.
- Users can **communicate with the recommendation system**, ask questions, and challenge its suggestions.
- The user now effectively has a **negotiation channel** with the system.

This change significantly alters user expectations and emotional responses.

---

## The Shift in User Perception

Previously, when recommendation systems produced an irrelevant suggestion, users tolerated it.

This is because the system was perceived as a **non-interactive machine**.

For example:

- You buy coffee from a **vending machine**.
- The machine unexpectedly gives you a bottle of cola instead.

In this situation, the user might feel helpless or mildly annoyed, but ultimately accepts it as a machine error.

However, when conversational AI is introduced, the situation changes.

Now imagine:

- You order coffee from a **store clerk**.
- The clerk hands you a bottle of cola instead.

In this case, the user will likely become **angry or frustrated**, because there is now a **clear entity to blame or negotiate with**.

The existence of a conversational interface transforms the recommendation system from an **impersonal algorithm** into something that users perceive as an **agent capable of reasoning**.

As a result, users will judge recommendation quality much more strictly.

If a recommendation system suggests songs that the user clearly dislikes, users may conclude that the system is **stupid, incompetent, or poorly designed**.

---

## The Limitation of First-Generation Recommendation Models

This shift means that existing recommendation models are no longer sufficient.

Most current music recommendation systems belong to what can be described as **first-generation recommendation models**.

These systems rely primarily on **user behavior history**, such as:

- listening history
- collaborative filtering
- similarity between user profiles
- large-scale behavioral statistics

The underlying logic can be summarized as:

> "People similar to you also listened to this song, therefore we recommend it to you."

While this approach can work reasonably well when supported by **massive behavioral datasets**, it has a critical limitation.

First-generation models **do not understand why a user likes a particular song**.

They only learn statistical correlations between users and listening behaviors.

As a result:

- The model may avoid obvious mistakes if enough data exists.
- However, it cannot truly explain or predict **the underlying reasons for musical preference**.

Large-scale behavioral data allows the system to **reduce the probability of recommending something obviously disliked**, but it does not fundamentally solve the problem of **understanding musical preference**.

---

## The New Requirement for Recommendation Systems

In the next generation of AI-driven music platforms, user expectations will change.

Users will no longer be satisfied with a system that simply **avoids obvious mistakes**.

Instead, they will expect a system that:

- avoids recommending songs they dislike
- **actively recommends songs they genuinely enjoy**
- can explain or justify its reasoning when questioned

This shift raises the standard for recommendation systems.

The system must not only **avoid failure**, but also **demonstrate understanding of musical preference**.

---

## The Role of the Music Preference Model (MPM)

The **Music Preference Model (MPM)** is designed to address this new requirement.

Unlike first-generation recommendation models that rely solely on behavioral correlations, MPM attempts to model:

- **how listeners perceive music**
- **which musical structures generate reward responses**
- **how listener characteristics interact with musical features**

In other words, MPM aims to answer the question that first-generation models cannot:

> **Why does a listener like a particular piece of music?**

By modeling the cognitive and perceptual processes involved in music listening, MPM seeks to build recommendation systems that can provide **more accurate, explainable, and reliable music recommendations** in the era of conversational AI.

---

# 2. Core Idea of MPM

The fundamental reason why first-generation recommendation models produce unstable or inconsistent recommendations is simple:  
these models do not understand **why a user likes a particular song**.

They can observe behavioral patterns, but they cannot model the **internal decision process of the listener**.

As a result, the system only learns correlations such as:

- users who listened to song A also listened to song B  
- users with similar listening histories tend to play similar tracks  

However, this does not answer the core question:

> What cognitive or perceptual mechanism caused the listener to enjoy that song?

Because the model lacks this understanding, its recommendations are inherently probabilistic and unstable.

---

## The Conceptual Solution

The solution to this problem is conceptually straightforward.

If the listener themselves could be represented inside the model as a **digital version of their musical perception and preference system**, the recommendation process would fundamentally change.

Instead of:

> a statistical model recommending music to a user

the system becomes:

> a digital version of the listener recommending music to the listener.

This idea forms the core theoretical foundation of the **Music Personality Model (MPM)**.

MPM attempts to construct a **computational representation of the listener's internal music evaluation mechanism**.  
This representation includes elements such as:

- perceptual sensitivity to musical features
- structural reward patterns in music
- attention allocation during listening
- preference formation mechanisms

In other words, MPM attempts to simulate **how the listener experiences music**, rather than merely observing **what the listener played in the past**.

---

## Self-Recommendation Instead of Statistical Recommendation

When the system successfully models the listener's internal evaluation mechanism, the recommendation process changes in nature.

Under MPM, recommendations are conceptually equivalent to:

> the listener evaluating songs using their own internal criteria.

In practical terms, the model becomes a **digital proxy of the listener's musical perception system**.

Therefore, when the model evaluates songs in a database, it is effectively performing the same evaluation that the listener themselves would perform if they had the time to listen to every song.

Because the recommendation process is based on the listener's own evaluation logic, the theoretical recommendation accuracy can approach **near-perfect alignment with the listener's taste**.

While real-world systems will never achieve absolute perfection, this framework dramatically reduces the randomness and instability that exist in first-generation recommendation systems.

---

## Discovery of Latent Musical Preferences

Compared with first-generation recommendation models, MPM also introduces another major advantage.

The digital representation of the listener can evaluate **vast music databases** far beyond what a human listener could realistically explore.

This allows the system to do something extremely valuable:

> discover music that the listener would likely enjoy but has never encountered before.

In other words, MPM is not only a system for predicting known preferences, but also a system for **discovering latent preferences**.

Through a combination of:

- listener personality modeling
- music feature analysis
- filtering based on historical listening data

MPM can identify songs that match the listener's underlying perceptual and reward structure.

These songs may belong to:

- unfamiliar artists
- unexplored genres
- previously unknown music scenes

yet still align strongly with the listener's internal musical preference system.

This capability allows MPM to function not only as a recommendation engine, but also as a **personalized music discovery mechanism**.

As a result, users can explore new music while maintaining a high probability of enjoyment, significantly improving both the efficiency and the satisfaction of music discovery.

# 3. Music Personality

In order to construct a digital representation of a listener, the model must first understand how humans perceive music.  
More specifically, the model must understand:

- how humans perceive musical information
- which musical factors listeners pay attention to
- what changes occur in the brain during music listening
- what neural and cognitive processes lead to liking or disliking a song

These factors together form what can be described as a listener's **music personality**.

In the framework of the Music Personality Model (MPM), music personality emerges from the interaction of four cognitive systems in the human brain:

- **Perception System**
- **Preference System**
- **Attention System**
- **Valuation System**

Each system represents a different stage in the process through which humans perceive, process, and evaluate music.

---

# Perception System

The definition of the perception system is:

> **What musical information the listener is capable of perceiving.**

The fundamental nature of music is the **combination and transformation of sounds**.

Individual sounds occur over time.  
When sounds appear at regular intervals, they form **rhythm**.  
When multiple sounds interact in pitch relationships, they form **melody**.  
When melodies and rhythmic elements interact over time, they form **musical structure**.

Therefore, musical perception can be understood as a hierarchical system consisting of three major layers:

### Layer 1: Fundamental Sound Layer
```
sound
```

### Layer 2: Temporal Organization Layer
```
rhythm
melody
```

### Layer 3: Structural Transformation Layer
```
structure
```

From the perspective of MPM, the essence of music lies in the **variation and interaction of these four dimensions**:

- sound
- rhythm
- melody
- structure

When humans listen to music, they are fundamentally **extracting information from these four perceptual dimensions**.

---

### Why Lyrics Are Not Included in the Perception System

It is important to note that **lyrics are not included as a separate dimension in the perception system**.

The reason is that lyrics are fundamentally **language**, and language itself is ultimately composed of **sound**.

When humans assign meaning to a specific pattern of sounds, that sound pattern becomes **language**.

Therefore, from the perspective of musical perception, lyrics are still fundamentally a **structured variation of sound**.

As a result, lyrics do not form an independent perceptual dimension within MPM.

---

# Preference System

The definition of the preference system is:

> **Which types of perceived information the listener prefers.**

Every listener has a different set of preferences.  
These preferences determine the **relative weights assigned to different perceptual dimensions** during music listening.

In other words, the preference system controls the **importance weighting of the perception system**.

Because listeners assign different weights to sound, rhythm, melody, and structure, different listeners may enjoy very different types of music.

For example:

A listener who prefers **sound and rhythm** may pay more attention to:

- vocal timbre
- rhythmic groove
- beat structure

while paying less attention to melody.

As a result, this listener may enjoy **high-rhythm genres such as rap**, where rhythm and vocal texture dominate, even if the melody is relatively simple.

In contrast, a **melody-focused listener** may prioritize melodic development over lyrical content.  
This type of listener is more likely to prefer music with strong melodic lines and memorable hooks.

---

## Why Music Genre Is Not Part of the Preference System

MPM does not treat **music genre** as a core element of the preference system.

There are two main reasons for this.

### 1. Music Genres Can Increase Indefinitely

Music genres are historically contingent and can expand indefinitely.

For example:

Before the invention of modern computers, **electronic sound synthesis did not exist**, and therefore genres such as **EBM (Electronic Body Music)** could not exist.

Only after digital sound production became possible did these genres emerge.

Therefore, music genres are **not a stable classification system**, and new genres can appear continuously over time.

If a recommendation system relies heavily on genre classification, the system would need constant updates whenever new genres appear.

---

### 2. Music Genres Cannot Fully Explain Listener Preferences

Musical structure determines how musical events unfold over time.
Structure controls the timing, density, and predictability of reward events.

A simple example illustrates this limitation.

The author of MPM (**user0**) is a listener who places strong emphasis on **melody**.

Because of this preference, user0 generally dislikes songs with weak melodic content, such as certain types of rap.

For example:

- **Kanye West – Power**  
  user0 rating: **2**  
  (Likert scale, 1 = very bad, 7 = very good)

However:

- **Kanye West – Stronger**  
  user0 rating: **6**

Both songs belong to the same **artist** and the same **music genre**, yet the ratings differ dramatically.

Genre classification alone cannot explain this difference.

---

### Structural Explanation

When the two songs are analyzed based on **musical structure**, the difference becomes clear.

**Kanye West – Power**

```
0:00 intro chant
0:23 beat enters
0:34 verse
1:03 chorus
1:24 verse
1:52 chorus
2:15 chant break
2:33 verse
3:01 chorus
3:23 outro build
```

**Kanye West – Stronger**

```
0:00 intro
0:11 beat drop
0:23 verse
0:46 pre-hook build
1:00 hook
1:24 verse
1:48 hook
2:12 breakdown
2:24 hook
2:48 outro
```

The difference is structural:

- **Power** builds energy through layered structural progression but has weaker melodic emphasis.
- **Stronger** repeatedly reinforces a strong melodic hook.

From the perspective of **musical structure and melody**, the difference between the two songs becomes obvious, which explains the listener's preference.

Therefore, MPM emphasizes **musical structural features**, rather than genre classification.

---

# Attention System

The definition of the attention system is:

> **Which perceptual dimension the listener is currently focusing on when processing musical information.**

When listening to music, the listener simultaneously receives information from multiple perceptual channels:

- sound
- rhythm
- melody
- structure

The **preference system** determines the listener's long-term weighting of these dimensions.

However, the **attention system dynamically shifts focus** between them.

From the perspective of musical construction, the typical order in which listeners process information is:

```
sound → rhythm → melody
```

When a significant structural change occurs in the music, attention may temporarily shift toward structural variation.

However, because of the psychological phenomenon known as **habituation**, attention will gradually return to the listener's preferred dimension.

For example:

```
sound → rhythm → melody → structure → melody
```

---

### Predictive Processing in Music Listening

Humans constantly and unconsciously predict what will happen next.

This predictive mechanism evolved as a survival system.  
If humans can predict potential dangers in advance, they can avoid them and increase survival probability.

The same mechanism operates during music listening.

Listeners continuously and subconsciously **predict the next moment of a song**.

When predictions succeed, the listening experience remains stable and comfortable.

However, when unexpected or potentially threatening sounds appear, attention shifts immediately.

In such situations:

- **arousal increases**
- **information intake increases**
- the **amygdala becomes highly active**

This produces emotional responses such as discomfort, fear, or tension.

For example, if a song suddenly introduces an **unpredictable and harsh sound**, attention may shift toward that sound:

```
sound → rhythm → melody → structure → melody → sound (sharp noise)
```

This causes a temporary redistribution of perceptual weights.

Example weight shift:

```
Sound:     4 → 7
Rhythm:    4 → 1
Melody:    7 → 1
Structure: 3 → 1
```

---

### Habituation and Structural Complexity

Human habituation requires time.

If a song changes its structure too frequently, the listener may not have enough time to adapt to one melodic pattern before another appears.

In such cases, attention becomes locked onto structural variation:

```
sound → rhythm → melody → structure → structure → structure
```

This prevents the listener from effectively extracting information from the sound, rhythm, or melody dimensions.

Therefore, in certain situations, the system must dynamically adjust the **perceptual weighting of dimensions**.

---

# Valuation System - Predictive Reward Hypothesis

MPM assumes that musical reward is generated when the listener successfully predicts upcoming musical events.
Music that repeatedly produces successful predictions and rewarding surprises tends to receive higher evaluation scores.

The definition of the valuation system is:

> **How the listener evaluates the musical information they receive.**

Some songs feel enjoyable, while others feel unpleasant.

From a neuroscience perspective, this difference is primarily related to the activity of two brain regions:

- **Nucleus accumbens**
- **Amygdala**

During music listening, listeners subconsciously attempt to **predict upcoming musical events**.

When predictions are successful, the **nucleus accumbens** responds by releasing **dopamine**, producing a reward signal.

For example:

When a song gradually builds toward a peak, the changes in volume and rhythm often follow predictable patterns.

Because these patterns are easy to anticipate, the listener can successfully predict them, leading to repeated dopamine release and a strong sense of reward.

As a result, the listener's evaluation of the song improves.


---

### Negative Evaluation

When predictions fail in a negative or threatening way, the **amygdala** becomes active.

This generates emotional responses such as:

- discomfort
- fear
- tension

These responses reduce the listener's evaluation of the music.

---

### Individual Differences in Reward and Punishment

Because every listener has different preferences, the conditions under which reward or punishment signals are generated also differ.

Therefore, the valuation system must adjust its parameters according to **individual weighting of reward and penalty factors**.

In MPM, this means that each listener's valuation system must be calibrated based on their personal music personality.

Only by modeling these individual reward and punishment patterns can the system accurately simulate how a listener evaluates music.

---

# 4. How to Embed a Music Personality into the Model

The fundamental difference between humans and computer programs lies in how systems operate.

Humans process information through **multiple cognitive systems that operate simultaneously and influence each other**.  
These systems interact dynamically and continuously.

In contrast, computer programs operate through **fixed procedural steps that execute independently**.

If human cognitive systems were copied directly into a computational model without modification, the resulting system would become extremely complex. Such complexity would make the model difficult to organize, maintain, and adjust during later development.

Therefore, the human systems must be **decomposed, reorganized, simplified, and adapted** into structures suitable for computational implementation.

In other words, although music personality is conceptually composed of four systems, the computational model does not need to maintain a strict one-to-one correspondence with those systems.

---

## Human Systems vs Program Representation

The music personality framework contains four conceptual components:

- **Perception System**  
  What the listener is capable of perceiving.

- **Preference System**  
  What the listener prefers to perceive.

- **Attention System**  
  What the listener is currently focusing on.

- **Valuation System**  
  How the listener evaluates the perceived information.

However, for computational implementation these systems can be simplified.

Within the model:

- The **Preference System** can be represented as **weights applied to the Perception System**.
- The **Attention System** can be represented as **events that dynamically adjust those weights**.
- The **Valuation System** becomes **a scoring function derived from weighted perceptual inputs**.

Therefore, embedding music personality into the model requires understanding the fundamental difference between:

- **how humans obtain information from music**
- **how programs obtain information from music data**

---

# How the Model Simulates the Human Perception System

The essential process of human music listening can be described as:

> obtaining information sequentially as the song progresses through time, while continuously predicting upcoming events and receiving reward signals when predictions succeed.

All listeners obtain musical information from songs.  
The information obtained can be divided into four perceptual dimensions:

- **sound**
- **rhythm**
- **melody**
- **structure**

Each listener assigns different weights to these four dimensions.  
Therefore, identifying a listener's perceptual weighting is essential.

MPM determines the listener's **perception system weights** through a questionnaire known as **LSQ (Listening Style Questionnaire)**.

However, an important limitation exists:

> Questionnaire responses represent **subjective weights**, not **actual perceptual weights**.

Subjective weights do not always equal actual behavioral weights.

Therefore, the subjective weights must be **corrected through calibration processes**.

MPM performs this correction in three stages:

1. **Initial calibration through matching with personality samples in the MPM database**
2. **Secondary calibration using the user's historical listening behavior**
3. **Final calibration through direct user feedback**

---

# Music Personality Samples

When a user completes the questionnaire, MPM performs the **first personality calibration**.

The questionnaire provides the listener's **subjective perceptual weights**.  
However, even the same listener will not provide identical answers each time they complete a questionnaire.

Therefore, subjective weights represent **an approximate range**, rather than precise values.

The MPM database contains **music personality samples with already calibrated actual weights**.

By matching the new user's subjective weights with the subjective weights of existing personality samples, the system can identify a **similar calibrated personality profile**.

This allows the system to perform a cold start recommendation using the calibrated weights of a similar listener.

In other words:

> After the first calibration, the system effectively becomes **a listener similar to you recommending music to you**.

Although every listener possesses a unique music personality, the number of required personality samples for MPM is relatively small.

If the four perceptual dimensions (sound / rhythm / melody / structure) are each divided into three levels:

- low
- middle
- high

Then the total number of theoretical personalities is:

```
3^4 = 81
```

However, some combinations are unlikely or practically nonexistent (for example, all dimensions being low).

Therefore, in practice MPM requires **fewer than 81 personality samples** to initialize perception weight calibration for all users.

This dimensional representation allows MPM to model listener personality in a compact but expressive space.
Importantly, this is a **low dimensional personality space approach**.

Afterward, as more users join the system, their calibrated personalities can be added to the database.  
This gradually improves the speed and accuracy of the first-stage calibration.

At present, MPM currently contains **one personality sample (user0)**.

---

# Your Playlist Is My Experiment

After the first personality calibration is completed, MPM will recommend **ten songs** to the user.

Among these ten songs:

- **Eight songs** are standard recommendations.
- **Two songs** are secretly inserted for behavioral learning purposes.

By observing the user's listening behavior toward these songs, MPM performs the **second personality calibration**.

Each time the user requests a playlist, the system includes **two calibration songs** designed to refine the model's understanding of the listener's personality.

By analyzing historical user behavior, MPM gradually improves its estimation of the listener's perceptual weights.

Currently, this system is not yet deployed because there is no user database available.

---

# The Advantage of Conversational AI

The greatest advantage of conversational AI is the ability to **communicate with the user directly**.

Through conversation, the system can obtain user feedback and use it to further adjust the listener's actual perceptual weights.

User weight information can be collected in two ways:

### Explicit Method

After recommending songs, the system directly asks questions related to the perception system.

Examples:

- "How would you rate the melody of this song?"
- "Which song had the strongest rhythm?"

These questions provide explicit feedback for weight calibration.

---

### Implicit Method

The system can also obtain feedback indirectly through **controlled comparison experiments**.

For example:

1. The system asks:  
   "If you'd like, I can recommend two songs you might enjoy."

2. The user agrees.

3. The system recommends two songs that are similar in **sound, melody, and structure**, but different in **rhythm**.

4. The system asks:  
   "How do these two songs compare?"

5. If the user replies that the first song is better, the model adjusts the rhythm weighting accordingly.

This process allows the system to infer perceptual weights without requiring explicit explanations.

---

# Humans Are Constrained by Time

When humans listen to music, they are constrained by **temporal experience**.

Listeners begin at the **intro** of the song and progress toward the **outro**.

If an unpleasant sound appears early in the song, the listener may stop listening immediately, meaning they never receive information from the later parts of the track.

However, programs analyze songs through **metadata and feature labels**, which contain information about the entire track.

This means the program always evaluates the **complete song**, whereas humans only evaluate **the portion they have heard**.

This difference can cause instability in recommendation systems.

Example:

- The first half of a song contains structures the listener dislikes.
- The second half contains structures the listener enjoys.

The program detects the presence of preferred structures and recommends the song.

However, the listener stops listening halfway through the song.

Since the listener never receives information from the second half, the result may be:

```
Listener score: 2
Model score: 6
```

To simulate human listening behavior, the program must evaluate songs in **segments**, rather than as a single unit.

---

# Simulating Temporal Listening

Because humans continuously make predictions while listening, listeners often form an initial evaluation very early in the song.

In practice, this often occurs within the **first 30 seconds**.

Therefore, MPM divides songs into four segments:

- **Intro**
- **Early section**
- **Middle section**
- **Late section**

Each segment produces its own reward signal.

By evaluating the reward generated in each segment, the model can simulate the **temporal experience of listening**.

---

# Risk Control in Recommendation

By analyzing reward distribution across song segments, the program can estimate **when a listener is likely to skip the song**.

However, an uncertainty remains:

The program cannot know with absolute certainty whether the listener will actually skip.

Therefore, the model requires a **risk control parameter** during recommendation.

Songs with high risk should not be recommended.

If the model recommends a song that the listener merely finds acceptable, the listener may consider the model functional but not impressive.

However, if the model recommends a song the listener strongly dislikes, the listener may conclude that the system is unreliable.

This can significantly reduce user trust.

Therefore, the model must follow an important principle:

> **Avoiding bad recommendations is more important than recommending songs the listener might like.**

Negative recommendations damage user trust more severely than missed positive recommendations.

---

# Digitizing the Valuation Process

After receiving musical information, listeners evaluate what they hear.

If the listener likes the information, the brain releases **dopamine**, producing a reward signal.

If the listener dislikes the information, the **amygdala** is activated, producing negative emotional responses.

Therefore, the human valuation system functions as a **bidirectional scoring mechanism**.

The model can simulate this system by adjusting scores according to the listener's perceptual weights.

For example:

A melody-focused listener may receive strong reward signals during a **hook section**.

By increasing the score assigned to hooks based on the listener's melody weighting, the model can simulate this reward response.

---

# Major Events That Change Perceptual Weights

Certain events can temporarily change perceptual weights.

For example, if a song suddenly produces a **harsh and unpredictable sound**, the listener's attention shifts toward that sound.

This causes:

- temporary reduction of weights for rhythm, melody, and structure
- increased focus on sound
- activation of the amygdala
- emotional responses such as fear or disgust

Instead of reward, the listener experiences **punishment signals**.

---

# Individual Differences in Tolerance

Listeners vary in their tolerance toward certain structural features.

For example, the **long intro problem**.

Some listeners are impatient and cannot tolerate songs with long intros.

If the intro generates insufficient reward, these listeners may skip the track.

Other listeners are more patient and willing to wait for the musical development.

Importantly, this phenomenon is not directly related to perceptual weighting.

Instead, it reflects broader personality traits of the listener.

Therefore, the **first personality calibration (personality sample matching)** cannot fully resolve this issue.

It can only be addressed during:

- the **second calibration stage** (behavioral data)
- the **third calibration stage** (user feedback)

by adjusting the listener's tolerance weighting toward long intros.

---

# 5. How MPM Works

The questionnaire mechanism can effectively solve the **cold start problem**, but when MPM is used as a commercial product, an important factor must be considered: **user friction**.

If users are forced to complete a questionnaire before the system can recommend music, strong user friction may occur. In such cases, users might abandon the system before they even experience the value of MPM.

This happens because the user's goal is not to complete questionnaires, but to **receive music recommendations**.

However, the author believes that questionnaires themselves are **not the real cause of user friction**.

In many cases, users are willing to complete very long questionnaires if they believe the results are valuable. For example, personality assessments such as **MBTI** can contain dozens of questions, yet millions of users voluntarily complete them.

Therefore, the real cause of user friction is not the existence of a questionnaire, but rather **the user's perceived value and motivation**.

Users care about the **value of the product**, which in this case is music recommendation quality, not the questionnaire itself.

If MPM presents the questionnaire before the user has experienced the value of the system, the user may perceive the questionnaire as a barrier rather than a benefit.

---

## MPM Operational Workflow

To minimize user friction, MPM adopts the following workflow:

### Step 1 — Start in Non-Personality Mode

When the system starts, it enters **Non-Music-Personality Mode**.

In this mode, the system recommends songs using:

- user behavioral history
- global popularity of songs
- collaborative filtering

This is essentially the operational logic of **first-generation recommendation models**.

This allows the user to immediately receive music recommendations without any initial requirement.

---

### Step 2 — Optional Personality Diagnosis Request

At an appropriate moment, MPM may ask the user a question such as:

> “If you'd like, I can help diagnose your music personality.”

If the user declines, the system simply continues operating in **Non-Personality Mode**.

Importantly:

- this request is **not repeated frequently**
- excessive repetition could create **user friction**

The system avoids pressuring the user.

---

### Step 3 — LSQ Questionnaire

If the user agrees, the system provides the **Listening Style Questionnaire (LSQ)**.

Through the LSQ, the system obtains the listener's **perception system weights**.

This allows MPM to perform the **first music personality calibration**.

At this stage, the system constructs an initial personality profile for the listener.

---

### Step 4 — Experimental Playlist Calibration

After the first calibration, the system recommends **10 songs** to the user.

Among these:

- **8 songs** are standard recommendations
- **2 songs** are experimental songs used for behavioral analysis


These experimental songs should be selected so that they are similar across most perceptual dimensions while differing significantly in the target dimension being tested.

For example:

song A
sound ≈ same
melody ≈ same
structure ≈ same
rhythm = high

song B
sound ≈ same
melody ≈ same
structure ≈ same
rhythm = low

Through the user's interaction with these songs, the system performs the **second music personality calibration**.

Each time the user requests a playlist, the system continues using this strategy to gradually refine the personality model.

---

### Step 5 — Conversational Calibration

During casual conversation with the user, MPM may occasionally initiate a **probing strategy**.

For example:

> “I found two songs you might like. Would you like to try them?”

If the user agrees, the system presents two songs designed to test specific perceptual differences.

This effectively creates a **hidden A/B test**.

By analyzing the user's feedback or behavior, the system performs the **third music personality calibration**.

---

## The Advantage of This Workflow

This workflow significantly reduces user friction.

MPM never forces the user to complete a questionnaire or perform any specific action.

Instead:

- the user always has the **choice to decline**
- requests are made **infrequently**
- calibration occurs gradually through normal interaction

Most importantly, MPM does not repeatedly pressure the user to complete the questionnaire.

Instead, it follows a **voluntary engagement strategy**, allowing users who are interested to participate.

This approach creates a more natural interaction experience and allows personality calibration to occur without disrupting the user's primary goal:

> discovering music they enjoy.

# 6. Summary

In an era where artificial intelligence is becoming widely integrated into everyday technology, companies will face **two stages of competition**.

## Stage One: AI vs Non-AI Products

The first stage of competition occurs between **products that integrate AI and those that do not**.

Products that incorporate AI capabilities will significantly outperform products that do not.  
During the early stage of the AI era, this advantage can be overwhelming.

However, this situation will not last indefinitely.

As technological progress continues, the cost of AI systems will gradually decrease. AI tools, infrastructure, and models will become increasingly accessible. As a result, the integration of AI will eventually become **a standard feature rather than a competitive advantage**.

Therefore, the author believes that during the **middle stage of the AI era**, nearly all digital products will naturally incorporate AI systems.

Once this happens, companies will enter the **second stage of competition**.

---

## Stage Two: Excellent AI vs Inferior AI

In the second stage, the competition will no longer be between **AI products and non-AI products**, but rather between:

- **high-quality AI systems**
- **poorly designed AI systems**

At this stage, the quality, intelligence, and design philosophy of the AI model itself will determine product success.

---

## Limitations of First-Generation Music Recommendation Systems

Current first-generation music recommendation systems primarily learn from **user behavioral history data**.

These systems observe patterns such as:

- listening history
- user similarity
- collaborative filtering
- popularity statistics

While these approaches can work reasonably well when large amounts of behavioral data are available, they remain fundamentally limited.

Because the information obtained from behavioral history is incomplete, first-generation systems cannot truly understand **why a user likes a particular song**.

As a result, they can approximate user preferences, but they cannot fully satisfy user expectations.

---

## The Second Generation: Music Personality Model (MPM)

Second-generation recommendation systems, represented by the **Music Personality Model (MPM)**, adopt a fundamentally different strategy.

Instead of simply **learning from human behavior**, MPM attempts to **become a simulation of human cognition**.

If a model can accurately simulate how humans perceive and evaluate music, then the recommendation process changes fundamentally.

The system is no longer:

> a machine recommending music to a human.

Instead, it becomes:

> a human-like cognitive model recommending music to the listener.

This represents the essential conceptual difference between MPM and first-generation recommendation models.

---

## Independence from Historical Behavioral Data

Another major advantage of MPM is that it does **not rely exclusively on historical behavioral data**.

Because MPM is built as a cognitive model of musical perception, it can perform tasks that first-generation systems cannot.

### 1. Evaluating Newly Released Songs

First-generation recommendation models struggle to evaluate **newly released songs**.

New songs typically have no listening history and therefore lack behavioral data.  
Without such data, traditional models cannot determine whether a user might enjoy the song.

MPM, however, can evaluate a song based on its **musical structure and perceptual characteristics**.

By performing reverse testing using the listener's perceptual weights, the system can estimate whether the listener is likely to enjoy the song—even if the song has never been played before.

This allows MPM to recommend **brand-new music immediately after release**.

---

### 2. Enabling Exploration of New Musical Experiences

First-generation recommendation systems rely heavily on historical listening data.  
Because they do not understand why a user likes certain songs, they often struggle to introduce users to new experiences.

In practice, these systems frequently recommend songs that are **very similar to what the user has already heard**.

More importantly, many traditional systems rely on **music genre classification** when generating recommendations.

However, genre classification cannot explain why a listener might give different ratings to songs from:

- the same artist
- the same genre

MPM avoids this limitation by focusing on **musical structural features** rather than genre labels.

Because of this, MPM can generate recommendations that cross:

- artists
- genres
- stylistic categories

while still maintaining high compatibility with the listener's preferences.

---

## Beyond Music Recommendation

MPM is not merely a music recommendation model.

More importantly, it represents a possible **direction for future AI development**:  
the concept of **personality-embedded models**.

If MPM can successfully learn the perceptual system humans use when listening to music, then similar approaches could be applied to other domains.

For example:

If the system learns the perceptual mechanisms involved in **watching videos**, it could recommend videos with similar accuracy.

In principle, personality-embedded models could be applied to many types of content consumption, including:

- video platforms
- news recommendation
- educational content
- entertainment media

---

## The Future of AI: Personality-Embedded Models

The author believes that the long-term direction of AI development will move toward **personality-embedded models**.

Such models aim to simulate the internal cognitive processes of users, rather than simply analyzing behavioral statistics.

By embedding personality structures into the model, AI systems can deliver:

- more accurate recommendations
- more natural interactions
- more personalized user experiences

As AI becomes a standard feature in digital products, the quality of these personality-embedded systems will become a key differentiating factor.

For companies competing in the AI era, staying at the **leading edge of this technological shift** will be essential for long-term success.
